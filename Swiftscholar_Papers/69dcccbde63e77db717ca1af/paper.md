# 1 Introduction

Image retrieval has been a fundamental task in computer vision and database management since the 1970s [37], serving as a cornerstone for various applications, such as face recognition [50], fashion retrieval [150, 156, 157, 212], person re-identification [104], and moment localization [121, 122]. Traditional image retrieval systems primarily rely on unimodal queries, using either text or images to convey a user's search intent [34, 137, 138, 142]. However, users often struggle to clearly express their search intent through a single text query or to find the perfect image that accurately represents it. To address these limitations and provide greater flexibility, Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) [180] emerged in 2019, which allows users to express their search intent by a reference image combined with a textual description specifying the desired modifications. By enabling users to utilize more nuanced search queries, CIR offers significant potential to enhance search experiences across domains, such as e-commerce [44] and internet search engines [86, 135, 183, 196].

The concept of CIR, which allows users to utilize a multimodal query to express their search intent, can be easily adapted for various real-world retrieval scenarios. For example, the reference image could be replaced with a reference video to enable Composed Video Retrieval (CoVR), or singleturn CIR could evolve into dialog-based multi-turn image retrieval. Since its introduction in 2019, CIR has garnered increasing research attention due to its potential value across various domains. As illustrated in Figure 1, the number of publications on CIR is increasing rapidly. To summarize the past and current achievements in this rapidly developing field, we present a comprehensive overview of work conducted up to June 2025. Existing studies primarily focus on addressing the following key challenges. (1) Multimodal Query Fusion. In CIR, the modification text and reference image play complementary roles in conveying the user's search intent. The modification text typically specifies changes in certain attributes of the reference image. For instance, given the modification requirement, "I want the dress to be black and more professional," only the color and style of the dress in the reference image should be changed, while other attributes of the reference image should be kept unchanged. Due to this nature, how to achieve an effective multimodal fusion for accurately comprehending the multimodal query poses the first challenge. (2) Target Images Matching. The semantic gap between the multimodal query and target images presents a significant challenge due to their heterogeneous representations. Additionally, the brevity of modification texts can lead to ambiguity. For example, the text "I want to change the dress to longer sleeves and yellow in color" could have multiple interpretations: the sleeves could change from sleeveless to either short or long, and the color could range from light to dark yellow. Such ambiguity suggests that multiple target images could satisfy the given query. Therefore, bridging this semantic gap and managing the one-to-many query-to-target matching relationship is crucial for accurate query-target matching. (3) Scale of Training Data. Training CIR models typically requires triplets in the form of <reference image, modification text, target image>. For each triplet, the reference-target image pair is often generated using a heuristic strategy, while the modification text is often annotated by humans. Creating such training samples is labor-intensive, which significantly restricts the size of benchmark datasets. Consequently, addressing the issue of insufficient training data to improve the model's generalization capabilities remains a great challenge.

Existing work in this area can be broadly divided into two main categories: supervised learningbased approaches and zero-shot learning-based approaches. The key distinction between these methods lies in the availability of annotated training triplets. Supervised approaches rely on annotated triplets from the dataset to train the model, while zero-shot approaches leverage largescale, easily accessible data, such as image-text pairs, for pre-training without requiring annotated triplets for optimization. To facilitate deeper analysis, we establish a fine-grained taxonomy for each category. For supervised CIR approaches, we summarize existing methods based on the four key components of the general framework: feature extraction, image-text fusion, target matching, and data augmentation. For Zero-Shot Composed Image Retrieval (ZS-CIR) approaches, we classify methods into three groups: textual-inversion-based, pseudo-triplet-based, and training-free. As previously mentioned, the concept of using a composed multimodal query can be adapted for various scenarios. Beyond the primary task of CIR, several related tasks also involve composed queries, such as reference image plus attribute manipulation, sketch plus modification text, and video plus modification text. Since these tasks are closely related to CIR, we include their recent advancements to provide a comprehensive review of the topic. Based on the type of multimodal query, we categorize these related tasks into five groups: attribute-based, sketch-based, remote sensing-based, dialog-based, and video-based. # 2 Supervised CIR

In this section, we first provide the problem statement for the task of supervised CIR, and then present existing approaches. Generally, as depicted in Figure 3, existing models involve four key components: feature extraction, image-text fusion, target matching, and data augmentation. The first three are essential components for CIR, while the last one is optional and aimed at enhancing model performance. Existing supervised CIR methods are summarized in Table 1.

# 2.1 Problem Statement

Given a reference image and its modification text, CIR aims to retrieve target images from a collection of gallery images. In the supervised learning setting, existing methods rely on training samples $\mathcal { D } = \{ ( I _ { r } , \bar { T } _ { m } , I _ { t } ) _ { i } \bar  \} _ { i = 1 } ^ { N }$ denote a set of such triples, where $I _ { r }$ is the reference image, $T _ { m }$ is the modification text, $I _ { t }$ signifies the target image, and $N$ is the total number of triplets. Then, based on the training dataset $\mathcal { D }$ , existing methods aim to learn a multimodal fusion function $f ( \cdot )$ that effectively combines the multimodal query $\left( I _ { r } , T _ { m } \right)$ and a visual feature embedding function $h ( \cdot )$ to ensure that the composed query and the corresponding target image are close in the embedding space. This can be formalized as follows:

$$
f ( I _ { r } , T _ { m } ) \to h ( I _ { t } ) .
$$

# 2.2 Feature Extraction

Existing supervised CIR methods primarily use established textual and visual encoders, which we classify into two types: traditional encoders and Vision-Language Pre-Trained (VLP) modelbased encoders.

2.2.1 Traditional Encoder. For textual feature extraction, commonly used encoders for CIR tasks include RNN-based and transformer-based encoders. Representative RNN-based encoders are Bidirectional Gated Recurrent Units (Bi-GRUs) [31] and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [69]. Specifically, existing CIR studies [38, 101, 107, 109, 110, 167, 188, 224, 225, 238] employ Bi-GRUs as text encoders to process sequences bidirectionally, enriching feature embedding by capturing context from both past and future tokens. Meanwhile, several studies [41, 77, 200, 205, 210, 226230] utilize LSTMs, which introduce gated mechanisms to the standard RNN structure, effectively managing long-range dependencies for modification text feature extraction. With the emergence of transformers [175], a growing number of CIR studies [5, 58, 67, 76, 86, 154, 171, 204] adopt transformer-based encoders, such as BERT [40] and its variants (e.g., RoBERTa [123] and DistilBERT [149]). These encoders leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture global context across the entire text sequence, enabling parallel processing and producing deeper contextual embeddings.

Similarly, traditional image encoders used in CIR studies can be categorized into CNN-based and transformer-based encoders. Many CIR methods [24, 38, 77, 124, 167, 180, 193, 205, 229, 232, 236] extract image features with pre-trained CNN-based encoders, such as ResNet [98], GoogleNet [163], and MobileNet [74], which yield generalizable feature embeddings by being pre-trained on largescale datasets like ImageNet [39]. In contrast to CNN-based encoders, which directly feed the entire image into the encoder, transformer-based encoders redefine image encoding by segmenting images into non-overlapping patches and employing self-attention to model spatial relationships. One commonly used transformer-based encoder for CIR [154, 174] is Vision Transformer (ViT) [42], which captures more nuanced visual details with a self-attention mechanism over image patches. Additionally, several CIR methods [76, 171, 204, 210] employ Swin Transformers [126], which adopt a window-based self-attention mechanism for local interactions within each window, reducing computational complexity. Typically, transformer-based encoders provide superior representational capabilities compared to RNN-based or CNN-based encoders, especially when pre-trained on large-scale datasets.

2.2.2 VLP-Based Encoder. Recently, advancements in vision-language pre-training have led to the prevalence of VLP-based encoders, owing to their powerful representational capabilities derived from large-scale pre-training datasets. For example, several studies [7, 20, 28, 116, 181, 192, 195, 205, 211, 241] adopt CLIP [140] for feature extraction, which leverages contrastive learning on large-scale image-text datasets and demonstrates exceptional flexibility across diverse domains. In addition, some studies [100, 128, 129] utilize BLIP [103], which unifies vision-language understanding and generation with a multimodal mixture of encoder-decoder framework. Moreover, BLIP-2 [102] has also been adopted in recent CIR studies [203, 206], which bridges the modality gap with a lightweight Querying Transformer (Q-Former) and achieves state-of-the-art performance on various vision-language tasks. Discussion. VLP-based methods, by virtue of their pre-training on large-scale image-text pairs, establish a robust foundation for multimodal representation in supervised CIR. As a result, they outperform traditional encoders in performance and have become the prevailing approach in the research community. Recently, several studies exploring the use of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) as multimodal encoders, such as E5-V [90], FiRE [71], and GME [235], have emerged, introducing novel perspectives for encoder architectural innovation that warrant further investigation by researchers.

# 2.3 Image-Text Fusion

After extracting multimodal query features, the key step is fusing image-text information effectively for precisely representing the input query. Existing fusion approaches fall into three groups: explicit combination-based fusion, neural network-based fusion, and prototype image generation-based fusion. 2.3.1 Explicit Combination-Based Fusion. This group of methods fuses multimodal queries via explicit combinations (e.g., transformed image-and-residual or content-and-style), as shown in Figure 4.

![](images/4.jpg)  
Fi. .Illustration of explicit combination-based fusion methods, including (a) transformed image-andresidual combination and (b) content-and-style combination.

Transformed Image-and-Residual. Keeping the image feature as the dominant component, this type of method achieves image-text fusion by learning two parts: the transformed reference image feature and the residual feature, which can be formally expressed as $g \left( [ i m g ; t x t ] \right) \odot i m g + r e s .$ . Here, img and txt represent the reference image feature and modification text feature, respectively. To enhance image-text fusion, various types of image/text features, including global features [20, 23, 180, 205, 227], local features [99, 124, 228], hierarchical features [24, 28, 77, 78, 86, 95, 193, 210, 229, 236], and decoupled features [17, 195, 232] have been explored. The function $g \left( \cdot \right)$ is a neural network that derives the parameters applied to the reference image to achieve the modification operation, and $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication. res refers to the residual offsetting information. Based on the method used to obtain res, approaches in this branch can be further divided into two major categories. The first category of methods, including TIRG [180], VAL [24], JVSM [23], MGF [124], DCNet [95], CLVC-Net [193], Css-Net [236], and CRN [210], directly fuse the image and text features to derive the residual offsetting information, i.e., $r e s = h \left( \left[ i m g ; t x t \right] \right)$ . The most representative method is TIRG [180], which designs $g \left( \cdot \right)$ as a gating function with a Sigmoid activation function to adaptively preserve the unchanged information in the reference image, and $h \left( \cdot \right)$ as a simple Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)-based neural network to fuse the image and text features and derive the residual offsetting information. VAL, CLVC-Net, and CRN share a similar spirit to TIRG, except that they utilize the attention mechanism instead of the gating function.

Another category of methods adheres to the formula expressed as $r e s = h \left( \left[ i m g ; t x t \right] \right) \odot t x t$ to learn the residual part. Typically, both $g \left( \cdot \right)$ and $h \left( \cdot \right)$ are scaled to fall within the range of 0 and 1. This strategy works on preserving the unchanged part within the reference image, while integrating information from the modification text that is to replace certain aspects. TG-CIR [195], DWC [78], AlRet [205], and EER [228] all follow this criterion to achieve the image-text fusion. Notably, some methods employ simpler fusion strategies where $g ( \cdot )$ and $h ( \cdot )$ reduce to identity mappings: MCEM [227] and Ranking-aware [20] set both as I, LGLI [77] uses $g ( \cdot ) = \mathbf { I }$ , while SAC [86] uses $h ( \cdot ) = \mathbf { I } .$ . Besides, instead of using element-wise multiplication to transform the image features, SAC relies on the attentional transformation to derive the text-conditioned image representation and then adds it to the modification text representation to obtain the fused feature, which can be expressed as AttenTrans $( [ i m g ; t x t ] ) + t x t$ . Content-and-Style. Building on the premise that "each image can be well characterized by its content and style" [17], methods like [17, 28, 99, 232] explicitly fuse image-text features in both content and style spaces. For instance, CoSMo [99] first modifies content via a disentangled multimodal non-local block, then adjusts style for progressive adaptation. In contrast, LSC4TCIR [17] and PCaSM [232] parallelize content and style modifications: (1) decompose the reference image into style/content features; (2) perform text-guided semantic replacement in both spaces; and (3) fuse the features via a combination module.

2.3.2 Neural Network-Based Fusion. Methods in this group rely entirely on neural networks to fuse the reference image and modification text without explicit combination operations. These methods can be categorized into four subgroups: MLP-based, cross-attention-based, self-attentionbased, and graph-attention-based approaches, as illustrated in Figure 5. MLP-Based. Methods in this branch [5, 79, 38, 106, 107, 109, 110, 116, 128, 181, 187, 188, 192, 207, 208, 236, 241] primarily rely on the MLP to learn modality weights for image-text fusion. For example, as pioneers in applying CLIP to CIR tasks, Baldrati et al. [8] introduce a classic multimodal fusion network, i.e., combiner, which integrates CLIP-based reference image and modification text features using MLP-based weighted summing and feature concatenation. This network has been directly adopted by subsequent methods [7, 9, 51, 80, 128, 187, 192] or refined in later studies [116, 181, 208]. In addition, DWC [78] first introduces an Editable Modality De-Equalizer to refine image and text features with spatial and word attention mechanisms, respectively, and then utilizes an MLPbased adaptive weighting module to assign modality fusion weights based on their contributions. Similarly, PL4CIR [241] introduces a multi-stage learning framework to progressively acquire the complex knowledge necessary for multimodal image retrieval and employs an MLP-based query adaptive weighting strategy for image-text fusion. TMCIR [182] introduces an MLP-based adaptive token fusion network that dynamically merges visual and textual tokens by computing token-wise cosine similarity. ENCODER [113] first proposes an entity mining and modification relation binding network that extracts visual entities and modification actions from multimodal queries, and subsequently binds them through relational associations. It further introduces an MLP-based multi-scale feature composition module to fully utilize these established entity-action bindings.

Cross-Attention-Based. CIR methods [41, 57, 70, 76, 77, 100, 101, 201, 204, 206] in this branch primarily adopt cross-attention to model the interaction between each word in the modification text and every local region in the reference image, thereby enhancing their fusion. Among them, LGLI [77] introduces a localization mask, derived from Faster R-CNN [143], as an additional input for image-text fusion, enabling precise local modifications over the reference image. Recognizing that the generated localization mask may not always be reliable, the authors additionally introduce a channel cross-modal attention mechanism and a spatial cross-modal attention mechanism to effectively localize the to-be-modified regions. ACNet [101] proposes a multi-stage compositional framework that sequentially modifies thereference image based on textual semantics. At each sage, the framework first enhances image features using a self-attention layer applied to the image's regional features, and then applies a cross-attention-based relation transformation layer to establish cross-modal associations between image regions and word features. To address comprehensive modification intent reasoning, IUDC [57] introduces a dual-channel matching model comprising a semantic matching module and a visual matching module, where a gate-based attention mechanism is used to fuse LLM-generated attributes of the reference image with the modification text for semantic matching, and a cross-attention mechanism is adopted for visual matching. In contrast, to capture fine-grained deterministic many-to-many correspondence between the composed query and the target, SDQUR [206] leverages the Q-Former module of BLIP2 [102] to achieve adaptive fine-grained image-text fusion between each word in the modification text and every local region in the reference image. DetailFusion [216] proposes a dual-branch framework comprising a global feature matching branch for holistic semantic alignment and a detail-oriented inference branch for fine-grained region-text reasoning, where a cross-attention-based adaptive feature compositor is introduced for dynamically fusing global and detailed features, thereby enhancing target image retrieval. Unlike previous VLP encoder-based methods that compose the multimodal query in a late fusion manner, CASE [100] introduces a cross-attention driven shift encoder based on BLIP's image-grounded text encoder, i., a BERT encoder with intermediate cross-attention layers. The image is first encoded by ViT and then injected into the cross-attention layers of the shift encoder, enabling early image-text fusion.

Self-Attention-Based. Unlike cross-attention, self-attention uses the same input sequence as the query, key, and value simultaneously when computing attention scores for each element. This allows the model to capture dependencies among elements within a single sequence. Methods [27, 68, 132, 154, 171, 174, 194, 211, 238, 240] in this branch typically feed the concatenation of the encoded reference image feature and the modification text feature into a self-attention-based network, like Transformer, to fully learn their interaction for promoting image-text fusion. Among them, AACL [171] refines the standard Transformer encoder with an additive self-attention layer, which utilizes the additive attention mechanism to capture contextual information and selectively suppress or highlight the representation of each token, thereby facilitating the retention and modification of reference image information. Treating modification text as an instruction, SSN [211] decomposes the semantic transformation conveyed by the modification text into two steps: (1) degrading the reference image to a visual prototype (preserving only target attributes) via an MLP network, followed by (2) upgrading to the final image via a Transformer network. Both processes are guided by the modification text. Contrary to the above methods that compose the multimodal query based on their extracted features, some methods [58, 67, 108, 114, 127, 170, 203, 244, 246] first concatenate the encoded reference image features and word tokens from the modification text and then feed the concatenated token sequence into a transformer-based model for image-text fusion. Different from the above methods that adopt a single fusion strategy, AMC [247] and SDFN [200] consider multiple fusion strategies, where the self-attention mechanism acts as only one fusion option, and incorporates a dynamic routing mechanism for adaptive image-text fusion. Notably, several methods [68, 132, 154] employ transformer-based foundation models to jointly handle varius fashion retrieval tasks (e.g., CIR and cross-modal retrieval) through multi-task pre-training, achieving strong performance across diverse tasks. Graph-Attention-Based. Unlike prior approaches, some works leverage graph attention to model fine-grained interactions between image attributes and modification text. For example, JAMMA [224] constructs an atribute graph using Faster R-CNN features as nodes and spatial relationships as edges, and introduces a jumping graph attention network to infuse semantic information from the modification text into the attribute graph, dynamically weighting text-relevant attributes. Finally, it adopts the gate and memory mechanism [105] to filter out redundant attributes, thereby yielding a more discriminative global query feature. In addition, GSCMR [225] incorporates the geometric information of the image for enhancing the cross-modal attention, and rectifies the visual feature under the guidance of the modification text with a multi-head graph attention network [177]. 2.3.3 Prototype Image Generation-Based Fusion. This type of method achieves multimodal fusion by directly synthesizing a prototype image that satisfies the requirements of the multimodal query, converting CIR into an image-to-image retrieval problem. For example, SynthTripletGAN [167] pioneers the integration of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) into CIR, where a triplet loss is employed for metric learning. In contrast, TIS [226] introduces a multi-stage GAN-based structure that embeds a retrieval model within a GAN framework. To learn a discriminative composed query feature, TIS uses two distinct discriminators: one targeting global differences between generated and target images, and the other identifying local modifications in the generated images. Unlike the above two methods that concentrate on the CIR task, UniFashion [240] focuses on developing a unified framework leveraging LLMs and diffusion models to enhance the performance of multimodal retrieval and generation tasks with mutual task reinforcement.

Discussion. In the landscape of image-text fusion for CIR, the three categories of methods are interrelated and adapt to different feature extraction backbones. Explicit combination-based fusion methods offer interpretable rule-driven fusion templates. They directly merge image and text features via operations such as residual offsetting [17, 20, 23, 24, 28, 77, 78, 86, 95, 99, 124, 180, 193, 195, 205, 210, 227229, 232, 236] or content-style recombination [17, 28, 99, 232]. These "handcrafted" interaction patterns typically work well for various feature extraction methods, including traditional encoders and VLP encoders. Neural network-based fusion methods take these concepts further. Instead of fixed rules, they use architectures like Transformers or MLP to learn implicit, data-driven fusion strategies, turning explicit combination logic into adaptive interactions. Some neural network-based fusion methods are bound to specific feature extraction backbones. For example, graph-attention-based methods [224, 225] typically rely on the image object features. Prototype image generation-based fusion methods diverge by reframing CIR as an image-to-image retrieval task implemented via synthetic prototypes. Though seemingly distinct, these methods align with the core goal of achieving effective multimodal representation. However, the additional generative framework will inevitably introduce retrieval latency. As a result, such methods may not be acceptable in pure retrieval scenarios. Nevertheless, they can find acceptance within unified retrieval-and-generation paradigms, as exemplified by UniFashion [239].

# 2.4 Target Matching

We first present fundamental metric learning approaches for CIR target matching, then introduce four enhancement strategies: negative mining, image difference alignment, uncertainty modeling, and re-ranking. 2.4.1 Basic Metric Learning. Existing CIR studies primarily utilize three types of loss functions: Batch-Based Classification (BBC) [180] loss, soft triplet-based loss, and hinge-based triplet ranking loss, for metric learning. BBC Loss. This loss function is the most widely used for metric learning in current CIR studies [79, 70, 77, 79, 91, 95, 100, 101, 152, 180, 195, 203, 208, 211, 228]. This loss function aims to bring the query embedding closer to the annotated target image embedding while treating all other target images in the same batch as negative samples, pushing them away from the query embedding. It is formulated as follows:

$$
L _ { B B C } = \frac { 1 } { B } \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { B } \left[ - \log \left( \frac { e x p \{ \kappa ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) / \tau \} } { \sum _ { j = 1 } ^ { B } e x p \{ \kappa ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( j ) } ) / \tau \} } \right) \right] ,
$$

where $\phi ^ { ( i ) }$ and $\mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) }$   
in the batch, respectively. $B$ is the batch size, $\kappa \left( \cdot , \cdot \right)$ denotes the cosine similarity function, and $\tau$ is the temperature parameter. Soft Triplet-Based Loss. This loss function is a variant of the BBC loss function, which selects one candidate image per batch iteration as the negative example. Widely adopted in CIR metric learning [70, 124, 127, 180, 214], it is formulated as follows:

$$
L _ { S T } = \frac { 1 } { M * B } \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { B } \sum _ { m = 1 } ^ { M } \log \{ 1 + e x p \{ \kappa ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) - \kappa ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \tilde { \mathbf { x } } _ { ( t , m ) } ^ { ( i ) } ) \} \} ,
$$

where $\tilde { \mathbf { x } } _ { ( t , m ) } ^ { ( i ) }$ $M$ is the number of negative cases for $\phi ^ { ( i ) }$ in one batch. Hinge-Based Triplet Ranking Loss. Adopted by several CIR studies [23, 107, 224, 225], this loss focuses on optimizing hard negative samples to address the redundancy and slow convergence issues of random negative sampling in soft triplet losses. Formally, the objective function is defined as follows:

$$
L _ { r a n k } = m a x [ 0 , \gamma - F ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) + F ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \tilde { \mathbf { x } } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) ] + m a x [ 0 , \gamma - F ( \phi ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) + F ( \tilde { \phi } ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { x } _ { t } ^ { ( i ) } ) ] ,
$$

where $F ( \cdot )$ denotes the semantic similarity function, $\gamma$ is a margin value, $\tilde { \phi }$ represents the hard negative for $\phi$ ,and $\tilde { \mathbf { x } } _ { t }$ represents the hard negative for $\mathbf { x } _ { t }$ . Most CIR methods perform metric learning using single-granularity features. However, given substantial scale variations in visual elements [126], several studies [24, 77, 107, 109, 110, 204] employ hierarchical matching to enhance target alignment. These methods typically: (1) extract multi-granular visual features; (2) integrate them with text features; and (3) apply granularityspecific metric learning. 2.4.2 Negative Mining. While the mainstream BBC loss helps models learn associations between composed queries and target images, it treats all other examples within the same batch equally as negative samples. This leads to two potential issues: (1) false negative samples, as in CIR tasks, a query might correspond to multiple target images, even though only one is annotated as the positive example; and (2) overlooking the varying impact of different negative samples in metric learning. To deal with the false negative issue, TG-CIR [195] first utilizes the visual similarity distribution between the ground-truth target image features and other candidate image features within the batch to regularize the model's metric learning. In addition, NSFSE [188] flexibly learns the boundaries between matched triplets and mismatched triplets using Gaussian distributions, where a flexible threshold is learned to distinguish positive target images from negative ones. Furthermore, SADN [187] first calculates the similarity between the composed query and each candidate target image to select the top $K$ most relevant candidate images as a neighborhood. It then adaptively aggregates the features of these neighborhood target images to refine the query feature. This incorporation of neighborhood target features effectively mitigates the adverse impact caused by false negative samples. To mine hard negative samples, i.e., negative examples that are particularly challenging to classify, and boost the metric learning, ProVLA [76] introduces a moment queue-based hard negative mining mechanism that uses momentum-based distillation to dynamically store the most recent embeddings of composed queries, reference images, and target images. These stored embeddings serve as hard negative samples for triplets, enabling the selection of hard negatives across multiple batches. Instead of constructing conventional query-level hard negative samples, MCEM [227] decomposes each query into two components (i.e., reference image and modification text) and generates component-level hard negatives in two ways: (1) replacing the full modification text in a training triplet, or (2) replacing only partial dimensions of the modification text embedding to create harder negatives. 2.4.3 Uncertainty Modeling. As aforementioned, the inherent ambiguity arising from general modification text often leads to many-to-many relationships between input queries and target images. However, in the existing CIR datasets, only one target image per query is annotated. To address this limitation, CIR-MU [26] adds Gaussian noise, estimated from the original target image feature distribution, to each target image feature, and dynamically adjusts the conventional one-to-one matching objective based on the fluctuation level of the injected noise. Differently, both Ranking-aware [20] and SDQUR [206] model data uncertainty by encoding both the composed query and target image as Gaussian distributions rather than deterministic features, and aligning the distributions of the query and target for optimization.

2.4.4 Image Difference Alignment. As mentioned above, mainstream methods model the task as a query-target matching task, i.e., encoding the multimodal query into a single feature and then aligning it with the target image. However, this learning paradigm only explores the most straightforward relationship within each triplet. In fact, beyond this query-target matching relationship, there exists a latent relationship between the reference-target image pair and the modification text. Intuitively, the modification text should capture the visual difference between the reference image and the target image. It acts as an implicit transformation to convert the reference image into the target image. Accordingly, several studies [91, 95, 101, 214, 230] explore image difference alignment, i.e., aligning the difference between the reference image and target image to the modification text, for boosting the metric learning. To achieve this, several studies [91, 95, 101] adapt the conventional BBC loss for image difference alignment as follows:

$$
L _ { B B C } ^ { ' } = \frac { 1 } { B } \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { B } \left[ - \log \left( \frac { e x p \{ \kappa ( \mathbf { v } _ { d } ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { t } _ { m } ^ { ( i ) } ) / \tau \} } { \sum _ { j = 1 } ^ { B } e x p \{ \kappa ( \mathbf { v } _ { d } ^ { ( i ) } , \mathbf { t } _ { m } ^ { ( j ) } ) / \tau \} } \right) \right] ,
$$

where $\mathbf { v } _ { d } ^ { ( i ) }$ and t() represent the visual difference representation of the ith reference-target image pair and the corresponding modification text representation, respectively. Typically, $\mathbf { v _ { d } }$ is derived from the reference image feature and target image feature by an MLP and a cross-attention network. Differently, JPM [214] adopts the MSE loss to narrow the distance between image differences and text modifications as follows:

$$
L _ { M S E } = \frac { 1 } { N } \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { N } \parallel  { \mathbf { v } } _ { d } ^ { ( i ) } - \mathbf { t } _ { m } ^ { ( i ) } \parallel ^ { 2 } ,
$$

Furthermore, to enhance image difference alignment, NEUCORE [238] designs multimodal concept alignment, which targets mining and aligning the visual concepts in the reference and target images with the semantic concepts in the modification text. Since the modification text is typically concise and contains limited semantic concepts, whereas images convey a wealth of visual concepts, NEUCORE employs an asymmetric loss [144] to supervise multimodal concept alignment as follows:

$$
\left\{ \begin{array} { l l } { \mathbf { s _ { i } } = s i g m o d ( \mathbf { v } _ { r t } ^ { ( i ) } \cdot \mathbf { w } _ { c } ^ { ( i ) } ) , } \\ { L _ { a s y } = - \frac { 1 } { N } \Bigg ( \sum _ { i \in \mathcal { P } } ( 1 - \mathbf { s } _ { i } ) ^ { \beta + } l o g ( \mathbf { s } _ { i } ) + \sum _ { j \in N } ( \mathbf { s } _ { j } ) ^ { \beta - } l o g ( 1 - \mathbf { s } _ { j } ) \Bigg ) , } \end{array} \right.
$$

where $\mathbf { v } _ { r t }$ and ${ \bf w } _ { c }$ represent the joint visual concept feature of the ith reference-target image pair and the embedding of one semantic concept extracted from the corresponding modification text, respectively. $\mathcal { P }$ and $N$ are the positive and negative sets, respectively. $\beta +$ and $\beta -$ are hyperparameters that balance the importance of positive and negative concepts, respectively. Notably, different from the above methods, MCR [230] formulates image difference alignment as a modification text generation problem. It inputs the features of reference and target images into an LSTM, attempting to generate the modification text directly. The commonly used cross-entropy loss for text generation is adopted to optimize this process.

2.4.5 Re-Ranking. As illustrated in Figure 3, most existing CIR methods adopt a dual-branch architecture: one branch encodes the query while the other encodes target images for retrieval, and utilizes the BBC loss to optimize the model by regulating the distance between the target image and each given query. This design enables efficient inference since candidate image embeddings can be pre-computed, requiring only the test query to be embedded and compared during deployment. However, this paradigm cannot guarantee the retrieval performance due to issues like false negatives and modification ambiguity. Therefore, several studies have proposed re-ranking techniques for boosting CIR performance. For instance, Re-ranking [129] introduces a dual-encoder to re-rank initial retrieval results obtained by standard CIR models: one jointly encodes the given query and each candidate target image, and the other jointly encodes the modification text and each candidate target image. Their outputs are fused by an MLP to compute final ranking scores. This re-ranking strategy enables deeper query-candidate interaction while remaining efficient, as it only processes a pre-filtered candidate subset. Additionally, VQA4CIR [47] re-ranks retrieval results by querying an MLLM, e.g., LLaVA [120], to determine whether the candidate images contain the desired attributes specified by the modification text. This method serves as a post-processing approach that can be seamlessly integrated into any existing CIR model.

Discussion. In essence, metric learning can be viewed as an art centered on the interplay among queries, positives, and negatives. Existing CIR methods mainly resort to established metric learningoriented loss functions (e.g., BBC loss, soft triplet-based loss, and hinge-based triplet ranking loss) for target matching. Its core lies in pulling queries closer to positives while pushing them away from negatives within the metric space. To boost the metric learning, many studies resort to negative mining, which aims to optimize the selection of negatives by ensuring they are both "negative enough" and free from false negatives. This is a critical aspect in CIR as annotated positives in datasets often fail to cover all actual positive samples. Uncertainty modeling, furthermore, serves as a means to model potential positive samples with Gaussian noise injection or Gaussian distribution representation. These approaches are not only applicable to CIR but also extend to a wide range of retrieval tasks. Image difference alignment methods offer another dimension of refinement by explicitly modeling the relationship between query-target image differences and modification texts. These approaches serve as valuable supplemental regularizers to standard metric learning objectives. Finally, while re-ranking methods can enhance retrieval accuracy by deeply analyzing the relationship between each candidate target image and the given query, they often incur additional inference costs. In fact, dialog-based CIR enables progressive refinement of retrieval results through iterative user interactions, making it more practical than computationally heavy re-ranking techniques. This aligns perfectly with exploratory search behavior, allowing users to gradually specify intent through successive refinements. We will present this research direction in Section 4.4.

# 2.5 Data Augmentation and Noise Handling

Existing CIR datasets typically comprise triplets in the form of $<$ reference image, modification text, target image>. However, constructing such triplets is costly and labor-intensive, severely limiting benchmark dataset scales. Moreover, manual annotation inevitably introduces noisy triplets, where the target image may only partially match or even mismatch the modification text. Consequently, prior studies relying solely on these limited and potentially noisy triplets have often suffered from overfitting, leading to poor generalization. To address the above challenges, researchers have proposed various data augmentation and noise handling strategies. In the domain of data augmentation, six representative approaches have emerged: —Image Replacement-Based. CLIP-CD [116] introduces a CLIP visual similarity-based data augmentation method that replaces the reference or target images in triplets with visually similar alternatives to generate pseudo triplets, effectively enlarging the dataset. Notably, it sets lower and upper similarity thresholds to ensure the quality and relevance of the augmented samples. — Image Difference Captioning (IDC)-Based. LIMN $^ +$ [194] introduces an iterative dual selftraining paradigm, in order to leverage unlabeled reference-target pairs in the dataset. It uses an IDC model [89] to automatically annotate image pairs, producing pseudo-triplets. In particular, in each iteration, it uses the CIR model trained in the previous iteration to filter low-scoring triplets, ensuring the pseudo-triplet quality. Reverse Objective-Based. CASE [100] and BLIP4CIR [128] expand the dataset by introducing a reverse retrieval task, i.e., retrieving the reference image given the target image and modification text. They prepend a special token (e.g., "[REV]" or "[Backward]") to specify the retrieval direction, encouraging the model to learn the shift vector between the reference image and the target image from both directions. — LLM-Based. IUDC [57] uses GPT-4V to extract attribute-level labels, combining them with TF-IDF [147] for identifying potential reference-target image pairs, then employs ChatGPT to auto-generate modification texts. SDA [215] first edits the original text via ChatGPT to create pseudo modifications, then synthesizes target images using GPT-4V conditioned on reference images and generated modification texts.

Query Unification-Based. To mitigate overfitting, DQU-CIR [192] bypasses pseudo-triplet generation but introduces two query unification methods to fully utilize the cross-modal alignment capabilities of VLP models. (1) Text-oriented unification combines the modification text with the reference image's BLIP-2-generated description to create a text-only query. (2) Vision-oriented unification embeds key modification words directly onto the reference image to form an image-only query. With the unified query, it reformulates CIR as either a standard text-to-image or image-to-image retrieval problem, compatible with VLP encoders. -Gradient-Based. GA [79] improves model generalization through gradient augmentation instead of raw data augmentation. It comprises two components: (1) explicit adversarial gradient augmentation, which introduces a gradient-oriented regularization term to simulate adversarial sample training; and (2) implicit isotropic gradient augmentation, which modifies gradients based on isotropy principles to increase triplet diversity. Regarding noise handling, three representative methods have been developed: — Noise Removal-Based. NCL-CIR [54] introduces a noise-pair filter block based on a Gaussian mixture model, which automatically identifies and filters out noisy triplets (e.g., partially matched or mismatched image-text pairs), while retaining valid triplets through a probabilistic thresholding mechanism for metric learning. — Noise-Aware Optimization-Based. Similar to NCL-CIR, TME [108] also uses a Gaussian mixture model to divide triplets into clean and noisy sets. Differently, TME reformulates noisy triplet correspondence as an adapter mismatch problem and employs visual variation modeling to generate pseudo-text tokens that align with authentic modifications, thereby enabling learning from both clean and noisy data. Furthermore, TME introduces a task-oriented prompt to replace irrelevant reference images, enhancing text-image alignment and mitigating overfitting caused by partially matched triplets. Data Refinement-Based. TMCIR [182] introduces a diffusion-based pseudo-target generation module, which synthesizes noise-free pseudo-target images conditioned on reference images and modification texts, thereby improving cross-modal alignment in the embedding space. Discussion. For data augmentation, current methods primarily focus on mining potential triplets from existing datasets [100, 116, 128, 194] or leveraging LLMs to enrich training data [57, 192, 215]. While these approaches show some improvement, their augmented data is limited and mostly indomain. Future exploration of large-scale open-domain triplet construction could further enhance model generalization. Regarding noise handling, recent advances have increasingly addressed the impact of noisy triplets, with strategies shifting from passive removal to proactive utilization (e.g., noise-aware optimization or pseudo-target generation). However, existing methods employ a rigid binary classification to divide samples into clean and noisy categories. Additionally, they treat all noisy samples equally, failing to recognize that some can serve as beneficial hard negatives to enhance metric learning. Thus, advanced noise-handling methods capable of fully leveraging noisy samples deserve further study.

# 3 ZS-CIR

Although supervised CIR approaches have achieved favorable performance, they rely heavily on annotated triplets in the form of <reference image, modification text, target image> for training. However, annotating modification text for each possible $<$ reference image, target image> pair is a time-consuming process. To reduce the dependence on labeled datasets, Pic2Word [146] introduces ZS-CIR, which aims to perform retrieval without requiring any annotated training triplets. Existing ZS-CIR methods can be broadly categorized into three groups: textual-inversion-based, pseudotriplet-based, and training-free. For ease of reference, Table 2 summarizes these methods across the three categories.

# 3.1 Textual-Inversion-Based

Methods [1, 6, 10, 29, 43, 63, 117, 146, 162, 165, 185] in this category begin by using text inversion technology [36, 52] to map reference image embeddings into textual token representations. These tokens are then combined with those from the modification text to form a unified query. The query is subsequently encoded using the text encoder of a VLP model, like CLIP, enabling image-text fusion. As illustrated in Figure 6, existing methods can be broadly categorized into two groups: coarse-grained and fine-grained textual inversion.

3.1.1 Coarse-Grained Textual Inversion. To realize ZS-CIR without any triplet data, methods [1, 6, 10, 29, 146, 162, 185] in this branch consider mapping the global features of the reference images into pseudo-word tokens to address the challenge of multimodal fusion. Pic2Word [146] is a pioneering method in this category, introducing the task of ZS-CIR. It leverages a collection of unlabeled images to train a lightweight mapping network that transforms image embeddings, obtained from the CLIP visual encoder, into token embeddings compatible with the CLIP text encoder. In this way, each image can be represented by a pseudo-token-based sentence, such as "a photo of $S ^ { * }$ ," where $S ^ { * }$ denotes a learnable pseudo-word. The mapping network is optimized using a contrastive loss between the image feature and its corresponding pseudo-token-based sentence embedding. Around the same time, SEARLE [6] proposed two approaches: an Optimization-Based Textual Inversion (OTI) method and a mapping network-based method, both aimed at learning pseudo-word tokens that encapsulate the visual content of each image. Unlike Pic2Word, these methods incorporate category-based semantic regularization to align pseudo-word tokens with the CLIP token embedding space, ensuring compatibility with real textual tokens. Building on SEARLE, iSEARLE [1] introduces Gaussian noise to text features during OTI to reduce the modality gap between text and image. Moreover, to enhance the mapping network's ability to capture visual contents, iSEARLE implements a similarity clustering-based hard negative sampling strategy, ensuring that each training batch contains a proportion of visually similar images. Furthermore, KEDs [162] introduces a Bi-Modality Knowledge-Guided Projection network, which leverages an external database to provide relevant image-caption pairs as knowledge, enriching the mapping function and improving its generalization ability. Additionally, similar to SEARLE, recognizing the challenges of aligning pseudo-word tokens with real text concepts using only image contrastive training, KEDs introduces an additional training stream that explicitly aligns pseudo-word tokens with semantics using pseudo triplets uncovered from image-caption pairs. To alleviate the task discrepancy between pre-training (where image and text are aligned) and inference (where they are composed), DeG [29] mines complementary information from image captions to enrich the semantics of the pseudo-word token. For modality gap reduction in mapping, CIG [185] proposes a two-stage mechanism: first mapping images to pseudo-words and generating pseudo-target images via latent diffusion models, then remapping these pseudo-images to enhanced pseudowords. By fusing original and enhanced pseudo-words, CIG produces more robust compositional text embeddings, bridging the gap between linguistic and visual spaces. In contrast, MLLM-I2W [10] tackles this challenge through dynamic fusion of visual features and MLLM-generated image descriptions to generate discriminative pseudo-words. Furthermore, MLLM-I2W replaces traditional MLP-based mapping networks with a Mixture-of-Experts [85] architecture, where task-specific experts are dynamically activated to improve generalization across diverse retrieval scenarios.

3.1.2 Fine-Grained Textual Inversion. Beyond the above methods, some studies [43, 117, 162, 165] have explored fine-grained textual inversion, which meticulously integrates both global and local features of the image, to enhance the performance of ZS-CIR. For instance, instead of converting the entire visual content of an image, Context-I2W [165] adaptively selects caption-relevant content for textual inversion using a context-dependent word mapping network. It employs an intent view selector to map the given image to a task-specific manipulation view and a visual target extractor to collect content related to the specific view. Instead of converting an image into a single pseudo-word token, FTI4CIR [117] maps the image into a subject-oriented pseudo-word token along with several attribute-oriented pseudo-word tokens to comprehensively represent the image in textual form. Additionally, it introduces a tri-wise semantic regularization method based on BLIP-generated image captions, aligning the fine-grained pseudo-word tokens with the real-word token embedding space. ISA [43], akin to FTI4CIR, converts an image into a series of sentence tokens rather than a single pseudo-word token. It incorporates a spatial attention mechanism-based adaptive token learner to select prominent visual patterns from the image. Moreover, ISA adopts an asymmetric architecture to optimize deployment in resource-constrained environments. While these methods exhibit promising generalization capabilities to unseen datasets, they rely on a fixed, pre-defined text prompt (e.g., "a photo of $S ^ { * * }$ )during training. This limitation reduces their ability to handle diverse textual conditions encountered in real-world applications. To address this issue, LinCIR [63] introduces a self-masking projection, which trains a language-only mapping network capable of projecting a given text into a pseudo-token-based embedding by flexibly replacing "keywords" (i.e., consecutive adjectives and nouns) in the text with the projected latent embedding of the text. By minimizing the MSE loss between the pseudo-token-based embedding and the projected latent text embedding, the pseudo-tokens of "keywords" effectively encapsulate the essential information of the input text. To further bridge the modality gap during inference, LinCIR introduces a random noise addition strategy, adapting the language-only mapping network to handle visual input seamlessly.

Discussion. Among the discussed methods, coarse-grained approaches (e.g., Pic2Word, SEARLE, KEDs) establish a foundational framework by mapping entire images to single pseudo-word tokens. However, this oversimplification inherently sacrifices fine-grained visual details, limiting their effectiveness. In contrast, fine-grained methods (e.g., Context-I2W, FTI4CIR) address this limitation by incorporating local image information into the mapped pseudo-word tokens, demonstrating superior performance. Despite these advancements, two critical challenges persist across all existing methods: (1) a fundamental misalignment between training objectives (focused on image-text alignment) and inference tasks (compositional retrieval), limiting generalization capability; and (2) irreversible information loss during pseudo-token mapping, degrading zero-shot performance on complex queries that require detailed information. Although recent efforts (e.g., DeG, CIG) have partially alleviated these issues through techniques such as knowledge-guided projection and pseudo-image fusion, both challenges remain open problems requiring further exploration.

# 3.2 Pseudo-Triplet-Based

These studies tackle ZS-CIR via automatic pseudo-triplet generation, falling into two classes (as illustrated in Figure 7): LLM-based and mask-based triplet generation methods. 3.2.1 LLM-Based Triplet Generation. To reduce reliance on manual annotation, several studies [15, 45, 62, 88, 92, 111, 125, 172, 173, 186, 189, 233] uses LLMs' reasoning capabilities to auto-generate pseudo triplets.

Image-Text-Based. Among these methods, TransAgg [125] is the first to use LLMs for generating pseudo-triplets from a set of image-caption pairs, such as those in the Laion-COCO dataset.1 Specifically, given an image-text pair, it treats the image as the reference image and generates both the modification text and target image caption based on the provided caption, using either a carefully crafted template or an LLM. Drawing inspiration from, TransAgg introduces eight types of semantic operations: cardinality, addition, negation, direct addressing, compare and change, comparative statement, conjunction-based statements, and viewpoint, which guide the modification text generation. It then uses the generated target image caption as a query to retrieve relevant images from the Laion-COcO dataset by calculating the semantic similarity between the target caption and each image's caption. These retrieved images serve as the target images for forming pseudo-triplets, alongside the reference image and generated modification text. Building on TransAgg's pseudotriplets, TSCIR [189] decouples the training process into: (1) image-to-pseudo-word mapping from image-text pairs, followed by (2) pseudo-triplet-based multimodal fusion. This two-stage design explicitly separates visual concept mapping from compositional reasoning. MCL [111] follows a similar pseudo-triplet generation strategy to TransAgg [125], but instead of using the target image retrieved based on the generated target caption, it directly uses the CLIP feature of the generated target caption as supervision for training the CIR model. In parallel, SCOT [88] adopts LLMs to extend given image-text pairs by generating both modification texts and target captions, and relies on the combiner [8] network to realize multimodal fusion.

Image-Based. Instead of relying on image-text pairs, HyCIR [92] generates pseudo-triplets purely from an unlabeled image dataset, such as COcO. The process involves four steps: (1) extracting potential reference-target image pairs; (2) using an image captioning model to generate captions for both images; (3) generating modification text using an LLM based on the two captions; and (4) filtering out triplets with low semantic similarity. Then, based on the existing textual-inversionbased method Pic2Word, it introduces an additional training stream that integrates the pseudo-word tokens mapped from the reference image with the modification text, forming a unified text query. This query is then used to supervise both the text query representation and the target image representation using a contrastive loss. Instead of relying on visual similarity, MagicLens [233] extracts potential reference-target image pairs by mining images from the same webpage, where implicit relationships often exist. It annotates each image with detailed descriptions, including Alt-texts,2 image content annotation labels,3 and captions generated by a large multimodal model, PaLI [22]. In addition, MVFT-JI [172] utilizes unlabeled images and an MLLM to generate initial image descriptions, which undergo semantic expansion to produce both modification texts and target image descriptions. The framework establishes two core training objectives: (1) text-to-image retrieval and (2) target text retrieval. In contrast, MRA-CIR [173] employs an MLLM to generate modification text for image pairs mined via similarity-based strategies, and then constructs pseudotriplets for training. Finally, PaLM2 [4] is used to generate open-ended modification text for each image pair based on their detailed descriptions. To ensure the generated modification text is logical, techniques such as instruction-following [35], few-shot demonstrations [14], and chain-of-thought prompting [191] are employed.

Text-Based. Inspired by the powerful generation capabilities of diffusion models, Gu et al. [62] propose a generative approach to construct pseudo triplets. Specifically, they aim to first generate text triplets, structured as $<$ reference caption, modification text, target caption $>$ using two strategies: (1) collecting a large number of captions from existing caption datasets and generating the target caption by substituting keywords in the reference caption, while deriving the modification text based on a randomly sampled pre-defined template; (2) generating text triplets by an LLM (i.e., OPT-6.7B [234]), fine-tuned with text triplets from existing image editing study InstructPix2Pix [13]. Subsequently, the reference and target images are generated based on their respective captions using a text-to-image generation model, e.g., StableDiffusion [145]. Notably, this work also develops a diffusion-based CIR method, CompoDiff, which can handle various modification cases and enable control over modification strength.

3.2.2 Mask-Based Triplet Generation. While the aforementioned LLM-based triplet construction methods efficiently generate large volumes of pseudo-triplet data, they often require excessive computational resources. To address this issue, some researchers have explored mask-based triplet generation strategies [19, 231], which are more resource-efficient. MTI [19] is a representative work in this area. Specifically, given an image-caption pair, MTI treats the given image as the target image and randomly masks certain portions of it to derive the corresponding reference image. The provided caption, which encapsulates the predominant content of the image, is then used as the modification text to help reconstruct the masked image back to its original form. In contrast to random masking, PM [231] introduces a Class Activation Map (CAM)-guided masking strategy [18] to better mimic the complementary roles of the reference image and modification text in CIR. Specifically, PM begins by replacing the first noun in the given caption with "[REMOVE]" to create the modification text. It then calculates the CAM matrix for the given image, identifying the regions most relevant to the masked noun. These regions are then masked. Unlike MTI, which uses simple color blocks to mask the image, PM replaces the masked regions with corresponding regions from another image within the same batch, ensuring the completeness of the reference image. Instead of random masking, PrediCIR [164] dynamically crops the original image to serve as the target view, thereby forcing the model to predict the missing target visual content. Compared to random masking, the cropped image is more readily interpretable by the encoder.

Discussion. LLM-based approaches, e.g. TransAgg, HyCIR, and CompoDiff, leverage the powerful reasoning and generation capabilities of LLMs to automatically construct pseudo-triplets from image-text pairs, unlabeled images, or pure captions. These methods excel in generating diverse and semantically rich triplets, which are crucial for training robust CIR models. However, they often incur high computational costs due to the resource-intensive nature of LLMs, limiting their scalability and practical applicability. In contrast, mask-based strategies (e.g., MTI and PM) offer a more resource-efficient alternative by generating pseudo-triplets through image masking. While computationally lighter, these methods face challenges in ensuring the quality and diversity of the generated triplets. For instance, the reference images generated through masking lack realism and are highly sensitive to masking precision. Moreover, the modification text in these approaches is restricted to describing the target image, which deviates from real-world use cases where the text should instead express modification intent relative to the reference image. This discrepancy can introduce data bias, causing the model to rely overly on the modification text for retrieval rather than learning the nuanced relationship between the reference image, modification text, and target image. Furthermore, the high correlation between reference and target images in mask-generated triplets restricts sample diversity, potentially hindering model generalization.

# 3.3 Training-Free

Given their flexibility and scalability, research increasingly focuses on training-free solutions for ZS-CIR. As illustrated in Figure 8, existing methods fall into two categories: (1) transforming the CIR task into a caption-to-image retrieval task that can be handled by pre-trained VLP encoders; and (2) directly leveraging VLP models' pre-trained embedding space.

3.3.1 Task Transformation. Methods [46, 94, 112, 159, 160, 166, 218] in this branch employ LLMs to generate target image captions, which are then used to retrieve the target image via the pretrained encoders of VLP models. For example, CIReVL [94] introduces a modular framework where a VLP model generates a description of each reference image. Subsequently, an LLM combines these descriptions with the modification text to infer the target image's caption, which serves as the basis for image retrieval. Due to the nature of CIR, where the reference image and modification text often involve conflicting semantics, the inferred target image caption may contain concepts that should not appear in the target image. For instance, given the modification text "change the dog to a cat," the concept of "dog" should not appear in the target image. To address this, GRB [159] enhances CIReVL by introducing a Local Concept Re-Ranking (LCR) mechanism to ensure the retrieved images contain the correct local concepts. Moreover, since CIR is inherently a fuzzy retrieval task—where the target image's semantics are not fully captured by the input query—LDRE [218] introduces an LLM-based divergent compositional reasoning approach. This method generates multiple diverse target captions instead of a single one, capturing a wider range of possible semantics within the target image. To complete the ZS-CIR task, LDRE also introduces a divergent caption ensemble technique to combine the CLIP embeddings of these generated captions and retrieve the target image accordingly. Differently, OSrCIR [166] introduces Reflective CoT reasoning to overcome limitations in manipulation intent understanding. By progressively (1) extracting visual details; (2) inferring implicit goals; and (3) filtering semantic inconsistencies, it refines the target description for retrieval. COTMR [160], meanwhile, adopts multi-scale reasoning: first integrating queries via MLLM to generate captions, then using CIRCoT to identify objects that should/should not appear in the target image. Diverging from the above approaches, IP-CIR [112] implements a novel framework that first transforms the multimodal inputs into textual representations with LLMs and then uses MIGC [243] to generate pseudo-target images for retrieval.

Discussion. Task transformation-based methods, such as CIReVL, GRB, and LDRE, leverage LLMs to generate target captions from reference images and modification texts, effectively converting CIR into a caption-based retrieval task solvable by pre-trained VLMs. While effective, these methods face two key challenges: (1) converting visual images into textual captions may discard critical visual information, impairing multimodal query comprehension; (2) relying on LLMs inevitably increases memory costs and reduces inference speed. Advanced variants like OSrCIR, COTMR, and IP-CIR mitigate the first issue to some extent through refined reasoning mechanisms (e.g. Reflective CoT, multi-scale reasoning) or pseudo-image synthesis. In contrast, pre-trained space mining-based methods (e.g., WeiMoCIR and Slerp) extract multimodal query features directly using pre-trained VLM encoders, fuse them through basic operations (e.g., weighted sums or spherical interpolation), and retrieve the target image via the same VLM. Although these methods eliminate LLM dependency—making them computationally efficient—their simplistic fusion heuristics and limited reasoning capabilities make them less robust for handling complex compositional queries.

# 4 Related Tasks of CIR

Beyond the primary task of CIR, researchers have explored various related tasks that cater to diverse retrieval needs in real-world scenarios. Here, we present five representative related tasks that involve different types of multimodal queries: attribute-based, sketch-based, remote sensingbased, dialog-based, and video-based. For ease of reference, we summarize the methods proposed for these tasks in Table 3.

# 4.1 Attribute-Based

Before the formal introduction of natural language modification-based CIR, another flexible image retrieval task—where the query involves a reference image alongside attribute-based modifications—had garnered significant attention. This task, constrained to pre-defined attributes, primarily finds applications in domains with structured attribute sets, such as fashion and face image retrieval. Zhao et al. [237] pioneered this task by proposing AMNet, which comprises a memory block for storing the representations of various attribute values. The attribute manipulation is achieved by directly retrieving specific attribute representations from the memory block and then fusing them with the representation of the reference image to retrieve target images. Similarly, ADDE [73] also incorporates a memory block for storing the attribute values. Unlike AMNet, ADDE devises an attribute-driven disentanglement module, which uses attributes as supervised signals to guide the learning of disentangled image representations. Through indexing the appropriate attribute repre sentations from the memory block, the disentangled representations can be modified by removing, retaining, or adding speciicattribute values, enabling effective attribute manipulation Recognizing that different attributes are correlated with different regions in fashion images, FashionSearchNet [2] employs attribute activation maps to extract region-specific attribute features. Each fashion item can be represented by a set of attribute features, and the attribute manipulation is accomplished by directly replacing the specific attribute feature. Sharing a similar spirit, EMASL [3] extracts different part features for the acquisition of attribute features based on the designed rules. For example, the upper part is associated with the collar, and the side parts are correlated with the left/right sleeves. EITree [115] enhances interpretability by organizing fashion concepts into hierarchical tree structures. Guided by this tree structure, EITree generates meaningful image representations where each dimension corresponds to a specific fashion concept. This structure allows for seamless integration of concept-level user feedback, such as attribute manipulation, into the interpretable representation of fashion items. Building on the success of generative models in image editing, AMGAN introduces an end-to-end generative attribute manipulation framework. This framework generates a prototype image that aligns with user-desired attribute modifications on a reference image to improve target image retrieval. AMGAN consists of a generator and a discriminator: the generator employs visual-semantic and pixel-wise consistency constraints, while the discriminator incorporates semantic learning for precise attribute manipulation and adversarial metric learning to enhance fashion search effectiveness. Focusing on face image retrieval, FIRAM [222] leverages GANs in its framework design. Unlike AMGAN, which generates a prototype image directly, FIRAM aims to learn sparse and orthogonal basis vectors within StyleGAN's latent space. This approach disentangles attribute semantics, allowing for independent attribute adjustment and preference assignment.

# 4.2 Sketch-Based

Many studies have focused on combining sketch and text descriptions to assist users in retrieving target images. Among these, TSFGIR [155] pioneers the exploration of complementarity between text and sketch modalities. It introduces a multi-modal quadruplet deep neural network that encourages both the input sketch and text to be closer to the corresponding positive target image than to any negative target image. Additionally, this work contributes a dataset consisting of 1,374 sketch-photo-text triplets for shoes. To model more explicit interactions between sketch and text inputs, TASK-former [148] extends the dual-encoder-based CLIP framework by incorporating a sketch encoder and two new pre-training objectives: multi-label classification, enabling the three encoders to recognize objects, and caption generation, which reconstructs the input text from joint sketch-text embeddings (computed as the simple addition of the text and sketch embeddings). For more complex scenarios involving rough sketches paired with complementary text descriptions, Gatti et al. [56] present a dataset comprising approximately 2M queries and 108K natural scene images. They also propose a multimodal transformer-based framework, STNET. Like TASK-former, STNET employs three CLIP encoders for text, sketch, and image modalities. However, beyond contrastive learning, it incorporates three task-specific pre-training objectives: (a)object classification; () sketch-guided object detection; and (c) sketch reconstruction. Distinct from the aforementioned methods, STD4FGIR [96] draws inspiration from textual inversion techniques by transforming the input sketch into a pseudo-word token, allowing the multimodal input to be directly encoded by the CLIP text encoder. To address the challenge of costly data annotation for sketch-based fine-grained image retrieval, STD4FGIR is trained using only sketch-image pairs. It treats the difference between the image embedding and the sketch embedding as a proxy for the missing text query and introduces a compositionality constraint to model this relationship. To achieve fine-grained matching between the composed query and the target image, STD4FGIR incorporates two innovative loss functions: a region-aware triplet loss for precise alignment and a sketch-to-photo reconstruction loss to enhance representation learning. It is worth noting that STD4FGIR can be applied to various applications, such as object-sketch-based scene retrieval, domain attribute transfer, and sketch+textbased fine-grained image generation. In parallel, SceneTrilogy [32] focuses on learning a flexible joint embedding capable of supporting any combination of modalities—sketches, images, or text—as queries for diverse retrieval and captioning tasks. Leveraging conditional invertible networks, SceneTrilogy disentangles the feature representation of the input sketch, text, or photo into two components: a modality-agnostic part and a modality-specific part. The modality-agnostic parts across all three modalities are aligned using contrastive loss to enable cross-modal retrieval, while the modality-specific parts are optimized for self-reconstruction, ensuring effective representation learning.

# 4.3 Remote Sensing-Based

In recent years, earth observation via remote sensing has experienced a significant increase in data volume, posing challenges in managing and extracting pertinent information. To enhance search capabilities with greater expressiveness and flexibility, Psomas et al. [135] introduce the concept of CIR into remote sensing and build a benchmark dataset. As a pioneer study, it focuses on the attribute-based CIR, which aims to retrieve target images that share the same class(es) with the given reference image and possess the desired attribute(s) specified by the text description. This work designs a training-free method, WEICOM, which introduces a modality control parameter for balancing the importance of normalized image-oriented and text-oriented similarities. Both pre-p .  , W et al. [183] study the remote sensing image retrieval with natural language-based text feedback. Recognizing that previous studies on CIR primarily focused on intrinsic attributes of target objects, while neglecting crucial extrinsic information such as spatial relationships in the remote sensing domain, Wang et al. [183] propose a Scene Graph-Aware Hierarchical Fusion (SHF) network. This approach incorporates remote sensing image scene graphs as supplementary input data for enhancing structured image representation learning and target image retrieval. SHF employs a two-stage multimodal information fusion process. In the first stage, it fuses features from the remote sensing image scene graph and the remote sensing reference image at multiple levels to comprehensively capture the visual content. In the second stage, the modification text features are further fused with the final scene features from the first stage using a content modulator and a style modulator, which effectively capture and apply content and style changes. Considering that existing VLMs struggle with processing structured scene graphs, LEmpo [209] leverages LLMs for pseudo-caption generation and scene graph interpretation, converting structured scene graphs into natural language captions to enable unified feature extraction and alignment.

# 4.4 Dialog-Based

The task of dialog-based CIR is proposed to address the challenge that users often struggle to initially express their intentions clearly or provide detailed descriptions of their objects of interest. Unlike traditional single-turn CIR, it allows users to iteratively refine their queries until they find a satisfactory item. This further necessitates models to integrate historical retrieval data with the current query to effectively locate the target image. Towards this end, DIR [64] frames the task as a reinforcement learning problem, where the dialog system is rewarded for enhancing the rank of the target image within each dialog turn. Instead of using a real user to interact and train model Show, Attend, and Tell [202]. The user simulator is trained on a newly collected dataset with Amazon Mechanical Turk, where both discriminative and relative captions are manually annotated within a shopping chatting scenario. Notably, since the data annotation for training a multi-turn simulator is quite expensive, the work only explored a single-turn user simulator. To enrich the decision-making process for retrieving the target image, CFIR [220] designs a three--a from three perspectives: (1) visual similarity between the composed query and the candidate image; (2) alignment between the reference-target image difference representation and the textual feedback representation; and (3) alignment between attribute representations of the candidate target image and the textual feedback representation. Meanwhile, CFIR contributes a large-scale multi-turn dataset, named Multi-turn FashionIQ, from the FashionIQ [196] by integrating multiple single-turn triplets. Inspired by the exceptional ability of Neural Turing Machines (NTMs) [60] in handling complex long-term relationships, FashionNTM [133] introduces a Cascaded Memory Neural Turi Machine (CM-TM) forulti-ur shi mage etrival Cmrsig a controller, individual read/write heads, and memory blocks, CM-NTM sequentially processes multi-turn queries with a cascaded mechanism, where each block's input comprises both the current turn's query feature and the output from the preceding block, supporting modeling the intricate relationships within input sequences. Beyond previous studies, IRR [190] proposes a generative conversational composed retrieval framework, which formulates the task as a multimodal token sequence with alternating reference images and modification texts in the historical turns. IRR aims to autoregressively predict the target image feature based on the historical session data with the GPT decoder [139]. To mitigate the scarcity and limited historical context in multi-turn conversational datasets, Chen et al. [25] automate the construction of FashionMT via a four-step pipeline: Image Selection, Caption Generation, Base Modification Generation, and Retrospective Modification Generation. They also propose MAI, a model with a two-stage semantic aggregation paradigm and cyclic combination loss, enhancing semantic consistency and cross-modal alignment by progressively integrating reference images, captions, and modification texts. Recently, LLM4MS [11], Fashion-GPT [21], and ImageScope [131] integrate LLMs into the retrieval system to realize dialog-based CIR. Specifically, LLM4MS employs a Q-Former in BLIP2 [102] to translate image information into textual pseudo-tokens and then adopts an LLM T5 [141] to take into account all query information for retrieving the target image. To adapt the T5 model for this specific task while preserving its knowledge, LLM4MS employs LoRA [75] techniques on the query and value matrices of all self-attention and cross-attention layers within T5 for efficient fine-tuning. Towards building a commercial fashion retrieval system, Fashion-GPT [21] integrates ChatGPT with a pool of retrieval models in the fashion domain for handling users' diverse retrieval demands. To leverage the multimodal collective reasoning capabilities of MLLMs. ImageScope [131] presents a training-free retrieval framework capable of handling various language-guided image retrieval tasks, such as text-to-image retrieval, CIR, and dialog-based CIR, by converting them into a unified text-to-image retrieval process.

# 4.5 Video-Based

CoVR aims to retrieve target videos for a given reference image/video and a modification text. Ventura et al. [178, 179] first introduce this task and develop models, named CoVR-BLIP and CoVR-BLIP2, by adapting BLIP and BLIP2 to the CoVR tasks, respectively. Since each video can be represented by sampled frames, these models are able to address both CIR and CoVR simultaneously. This work also contributes a dataset named WebVid-CoVR with 1.6 million triplets by leveraging an LLM to automatically generate the modification text for two similar videos, identified by similar captions. One key issue of this dataset is that its sample modifications are mainly regarding static color/shape/object changes, which do not need temporal understanding. To address this issue, Hummel et al. [82] introduce EgoCVR, a manually curated dataset with 2,295 queries. Additionally, they present a train-free method TF-CVR, similar to the previous CIR model CIReVL [94]. TF-CVR involves a video captioning model to generate a caption for the reference video. Then it uses an LLM to combine the video caption and modification text to obtain the target video caption and employs an existing text-to-video model to perform the cross-modal retrieval of target videos. To avoid selecting semantically similar but visually unrelated videos, they introduce a similarity-based filtering strategy to first narrow the scope of candidate videos for TF-CVR to rank. To mine the rich query-specific context for promoting the target video retrieval, ECDE [169] introduces a novel CoVR framework. This framework uses detailed language descriptions of the given reference video derived from a multi-modal conversation model as the additional input to explicitly encode query-specific contextual information. For discriminative embedding learning, ECDE not only aligns the joint multimodal embedding of the input query with the conventional visual embedding of the target video, but also aligns that with the text embedding and vision-text joint embedding of the target video, respectively. To address the limitations of datasets like WebVid-CoVR in modeling finegrained intent, Wu et al. [221] propose FineCVR-1M, a 1M-triplet dataset with structured textual descriptions that decompose video semantics into retained, injected, and excluded features, reducing visual redundancy and improving retrieval precision. They also introduce FDCA, a framework with dual-level text disentanglement for more discriminative representations.

Given that CIRCO is specifically designed for evaluating zero-shot cross-image retrieval models, it contains 220 queries for model validation, and 800 for testing. Each query includes a reference image, modification text, and an average of 4.53 ground truth target images. Utilizing all 120K images of COCO as the index set, CIRCO provides a vastly larger number of distractors compared to the 2K images in the CIRR test set. GeneCIS [176]. This is an open-domain CIR dataset that serves as a benchmark for evaluating conditional similarity tasks. This dataset includes four subsets: Focus Attribute, Change Attribute, Focus Object, and Change Object, representing four different tasks. Among these, Focus Object and Change Object are constructed based on the COCO [118] dataset, while Focus Attribute and Change Attribute are constructed based on the VAW [134] dataset. Unlike other open-domain datasets, CIRR and CIRCO, which provide modification text, GeneCIS provides a single object name or attribute as the retrieval condition. To reduce the impact of false negatives, a gallery was selected for each triplet as the retrieval candidate set, with gallery sizes ranging from 10 to 15 images. This dataset consists of ${ \sim } 8 \mathrm { K }$ triplets.

Fashion200K [66]. This dataset comprises ${ \sim } 2 0 0 \mathrm { K }$ clothing images, categorized into five types: dress, top, pants, skirt, and jacket. Each image comes with a compact attribute-like product description, such as "black biker jacket." The dataset is divided into three parts: ${ \sim } 1 7 2 \mathrm { K }$ images for the training set, ${ \sim } 1 2 \mathrm { K }$ images for the validation set, and $\mathrm { \sim } 2 5 \mathrm { K }$ images for the test set. To construct triplets suitable for the CIR task, existing works [180, 193] first create image pairs by identiying only one-word differences in their descriptions. The modification text is generated using templates that incorporate the differing words, such as "replace red with green." Based on this construction method, there are ${ \sim } 1 7 2 \mathrm { K }$ triplets available for training and ${ \sim } 3 3 \mathrm { K }$ triplets for evaluation. MIT-States [84]. This dataset features a diverse collection of objects, scenes, and materials in various transformed states. It contains ${ \sim } 5 3 \mathrm { K }$ images, in which each image is tagged with an adjective or state label and a noun or object label (., "new camer" or cooked beef".The dataset encompasses 245 nouns and 115 adjectives, with each noun being modified by roughly 9 different adjectives on average. Following [180], image pairs that share the same object labels but with different attributes or states can be selected as the reference and target images, and the different attributes or states serve as the modification text. To evaluate the model's capacity for handling unseen objects, 49 nouns are used for the test, and the rest is for training. CSS [180]. This dataset is constructed using the CLEVR toolkit [93] to generate synthesized images in a 3-by-3 grid scene, showcasing objects with variations in Color, Shape, and Size. Each image is available in both a simplified 2D blob version and a detailed 3D rendered version. The dataset comprises ${ \sim } 1 6 \mathrm { K }$ queries for training and $\mathord { \sim } 1 6 \mathrm { K }$ queries for testing. Each query consists of a reference image (2D or 3D) and a modification text, and the target image. Notably, modification texts fall into three categories: adding, removing, or changing object attributes. SynthTriplets18M [62]. Distinct from datasets relying on human annotation, SynthTriplets18M uses diffusion models to automatically create ${ \sim } 1 8 \mathrm { M }$ triplets consisting of reference images, modification instructions, and target images. Adopting the approach of Instruct Pix2Pix, the dataset initially creates caption triplets by modifying reference captions with specific instructions and then converts these triplets into image-based triplets using diffusion models, resulting in highly diverse and realistic data. The dataset encompasses a broad spectrum of keywords, enhancing its suitability for open-domain CIR tasks, and provides rich textual prompts. This automated generation process enables the creation of rare and diverse image triplets that might not commonly appear in reality. LaSCo [100]. This is a large-scale, open-domain dataset consisting of natural images, specifically designed for the CIR task. Created with minimal human effort by leveraging the VQA 2.0 dataset [59], LaSCo leverages "complementary" image pairs, which are similar images that yield different answers to the same question, and transforms these question-answer pairs into valid transition texts using GPT-3. By exploiting transition symmetry, the dataset has amassed ${ \sim } 1 2 1 . 5 \mathrm { K }$ images and ${ \sim } 3 8 9 . 3 \mathrm { K }$ image pairs, which are then organized into triplets. Shopping100k [3]. This dataset consists of ${ \sim } 1 0 1 . 0 \mathrm { K }$ pure clothing images, characterized by 12 attributes with 151 possible atribute values. Following [73], pairs of images that differ by only one or two attributes are utilized as reference and target images. These differing attributes serve as modification attributes, facilitating the construction of triplet data for attribute-based CIR tasks. This approach results in ${ \sim } 9 5 4 . 1 \mathrm { K }$ training triplets and ${ \sim } 1 1 8 . 3 \mathrm { K }$ testing triplets, forming a robust foundation for developing and evaluating CIR models focused on nuanced fashion attributes. WebVid-CoVR. This is a large-scale dataset designed for CoVR, containing ${ \sim } 1 . 6  { \mathrm { M } }$ triplets. Each video in the dataset averages 16.8 seconds in duration, and the modification texts consist of roughly 4.8 words. With each target video linked to about 12.7 triplets, the dataset offers rich contextual variations essential for effectively training CoVR models. To ensure robust evaluation, they further introduce a meticulously curated test set known as WebVid-CoVR-Test, which is manually annotated and consists of ${ \sim } 2 . 5 \mathrm { K }$ triplets. FS-COCO [33]. This is a large-scale dataset of freehand scene sketches paired with textual descriptions and corresponding images, designed to advance research in fine-grained scene sketch understanding. It consists of ${ \sim } 1 0 \mathrm { K }$ unique sketches drawn by non-experts, with ${ \sim } 7 \mathrm { K }$ for training and ${ \sim } 3 \mathrm { K }$ for testing, each matched with a photo from the MS-COCO dataset and a descriptive caption. FS-COCO includes over 90 object categories from the COCO-stuff [16] and provides temporal order information of strokes, which enables detailed studies on scene abstraction and the salience of early vs. late strokes. This dataset serves as a benchmark for fine-grained image retrieval, sketch-based captioning, and understanding the complementary information between sketches and text. SketchyCOCO [53]. This is a large-scale composite dataset tailored for the task of automatic image generation from scene-level freehand sketches. Built on the COCO-stuff dataset [16], it includes ${ \sim } 1 4 \mathrm { K }$ unique scene-level sketches paired with corresponding images and textual descriptions, organized into ${ \sim } 1 4 \mathrm { K }$ sketch-text-image triplets, with $8 0 \%$ for training and the remaining $2 0 \%$ for testing. Additionally, the dataset includes $\sim 2 0 \mathrm { K }$ triplet examples of foreground sketches, images, and edge maps covering 14 classes, along with ${ \sim } 2 7 \mathrm { K }$ background sketch-image pairs covering 3 classes. This layered structure facilitates detailed studies in scene-level sketch-based generation and provides five-tuple data for comprehensive training in both foreground and background synthesis tasks.

Composite Sketch $^ +$ Text-Based Image Retrieval (CSTBIR) [56]. The CSTBIR dataset is a multimodal dataset specifically designed for image retrieval using sketches and partial text descriptions. It includes ${ \sim } 1 0 8 \mathrm { K }$ natural scene images and ${ \sim } 2 \mathrm { M }$ annotated triplets, each containing a reference sketch, a partial text description, and a target image. The natural images and text descriptions are sourced from the Visual Genome dataset [97], while the sketches are hand-drawn from the Quick, Draw! dataset [65]. By intersecting object categories between Visual Genome and Quick, Draw!, the dataset contains 258 intersecting object classes and is divided into training, validation, and testing sets aligned with Visual Genome's splits. The training set comprises ${ \sim } 9 7 \mathrm { K }$ images, ${ \sim } 4 8 4 \mathrm { K }$ sketches, and ${ \sim } 1 . 8 9 \mathrm { M }$ queries. Additionally, CSTBIR includes three distinct test sets: Test-1K, Test-5K, and an Open-Category set. Among them, only the Open-Category test set is designed to evaluate model performance on novel object categories not present during training, which contains 70 novel object categories (of which 50 are "difficult-to-name") and corresponding sketches. PATTERNCOM. This is a new benchmark designed for evaluating remote sensing CIR methods, based on the PatternNet dataset [245], which is a large-scale, high-resolution remote sensing image collection. PATTERNCOM focuses on selected classes from PatternNet, incorporating query images and corresponding text descriptions that define relevant attributes for each class. For example, the "swimming pools" category includes text queries specifying shapes such as "rectangular," "oval," and "kidney-shaped." The dataset encompasses six attributes, with each attribute linked to up to four different classes and two to five values per class. Overal, PATTERNCOM contains over 21K queries, with positive matches ranging from 2 to 1,345 per query.

Airplane, Tennis, and WHIRT [183]. These datasets are curated for remote sensing CIR. Each dataset is organized in terms of quintets, consisting of a reference RS image and its scene graph, a target RS image and its scene graph, and a pair of modifier sentences. The Airplane dataset comprises 1,600 remote sensing images from UCM [213], PatternNet [245], and NWPU-RESISC45 [30], along with 3,461 pairs of modifier sentences that describe differences in airplane attributes and spatial relationships between airplanes and other objects. The Tennis dataset includes 1,200 images featuring tennis courts and 1,924 manually annotated modifier sentence pairs. It emphasizes target and non-target spatial relationships while ignoring relationships between non-target objects. The WHIRT dataset is the most extensive, consisting of 4,940 images from WHDLD [151] and 3,344 reference-target image pairs. Its scene graphs provide comprehensive details on object attributes and spatial relationships, accommodating complex remote sensing scenarios. Each dataset features high-quality annotations by domain experts, serving as robust benchmarks for advanced retrieval tasks in intricate remote sensing environments. Multi-turn FashionIQ [220]. This is an extension of the original FashionIQ dataset [196], designed to model user interactions in a multi-turn setting for fashion product retrieval. It contains ${ \sim } 1 1 . 5 \mathrm { K }$ sessions structured as multi-turn interactions, across three clothing types: dress, shirt, and top and tee. Each session consists of multiple reference images, modification texts, and a target image, with turns ranging from 3 to 5. The sessions are constructed by linking single-turn triplets from FashionIQ, matching the target image of one triplet to the reference image of another, thus forming coherent multi-turn sequences. Additionally, the dataset expands the original attribute data to ensure comprehensive coverage, associating each target image with attributes like texture, fabric, shape, part, and style.

# 5.2 Metric

Recall Recall is a widely used metric in CIR to evaluate the effectiveness of retrieval systems. It is often denoted as Recall $@ k$ $( \mathrm { R } @ k )$ , measuring the proportion of queries for which the correct target image is retrieved within the top $k$ results. Recall $@ k$ can be defined by the following formula:

$$
\mathrm { R e c a l l } @ k = \frac { 1 } { Q } \sum _ { q = 1 } ^ { Q } \frac { \vert \mathcal { R } _ { q } \cap \mathcal { D } _ { q } ^ { k } \vert } { \vert \mathcal { R } _ { q } \vert } ,
$$

where $Q$ denotes the total number of queries, $\mathcal { R } _ { q }$ is the set of all relevant target images for each query $q$ . $\mathcal { D } _ { q } ^ { k }$ is the set of the top $k$ retrieved items for query $q$ $| \mathcal { R } _ { q } \cap \mathcal { D } _ { q } ^ { k } |$ represents the number of target images found in the top $k$ results, and $| \mathcal { R } _ { q } |$ represents the total number of target images for query $q$ Notably, in most existing CIR datasets, each query typically corresponds to only one target image, i.e., $| \mathcal { R } _ { q } | = 1$ , except CIRCO. Additionally, CIRR [127] further defines $\mathrm { R e c a l l } _ { s u b s e t } @ k$ to assess how frequently the desired target image appears in the top $k$ results when considering only a specific subset of images. Mean Average Precision. Mean Average Precision at $k \left( \mathrm { m A P } @ k \right)$ is a crucial metric for evaluating retrieval systems, particularly in cases where there are multiple relevant items. Initially employed in CIRCO [6], this metric integrates precision across various ranks to yield a single, averaged indicator of the system's effectiveness in retrieving relevant items. The formula for $\mathbf { m A P @ } k$ is given by:

$$
\mathrm { m A P } @ k = \frac { 1 } { Q } \sum _ { q = 1 } ^ { Q } \frac { 1 } { \mathrm { m i n } ( k , \mathcal { R } _ { q } ) } \sum _ { k = 1 } ^ { k } P @ k \cdot \mathrm { r e l } @ k ,
$$

where $\mathrm { P @ } k$ is the precision at rank $k$ , rel $@ k$ is a relevance function. The relevance function is an indicator function that equals 1 if the image at rank $k$ is labeled as positive and equals 0 otherwise.

From these tables, we obtain the following observations. (1) VLP encoder-based methods generally outperform traditional encoder-based methods by a significant margin. This is because VLP encoders are usually larger in scale and pre-trained on extensive image-text corpora through contrastive learning, endowing them with excellent multimodal alignment and cross-modal retrieval capabilities, which are essential for CIR. This can be illustrated by methods that adopt the same fusion strategy but different encoders, such as AlRet (CLIP-RN50) with a VLP encoder, achieving much better performance than AlRet (RN50) with a traditional encoder, as seen in Tables 57. Even when using the same type of encoder, the version with larger parameters typically performs better, as shown by CRN (Swin-Large) outperforming CRN (Swin-Base) (Tables 5 and 7) and FashionERN (CLIP-ViT-B/16) surpassing FashionERN (CLIP-RN50x4) (Table 6). These findings underscore that the choice of image and text encoders plays a crucial role in CIR, often outweighing the importance of image-text fusion and target matching method design. (2) Regarding image-text fusion, various strategies have proven effective. By summarizing the top-performing methods across the FashionIQ-VAL, FashionIQ-ori, Fashion200k, Shoes, CIRR, MIT-States, and CSS datasets and evaluating only prototype methods (with additional modules like data augmentation and re-ranking disabled), we find that DQU-CIR (MLP-Based), FineCIR (Self-Attention-Based), NCL-CIR (MLP-Based), and GSCMR (Graph-Attention-Based) achieve the best results. This indicates that while image-text fusion is a vital component of CIR methods, it is not the sole determinant of final performance, and identifying a universally optimal fusion strategy remains challenging. (3) Models incorporating additional target matching techniques or data augmentation modules consistently outperform their original versions. For instance, SPRC-VQA, which uses visual question answering to re-rank the retrieval list, outperforms SPRC as demonstrated in Tables 6 and 8; LIMN $^ +$ attains better outcomes than LIMN by leveraging augmented pseudo-triplets for iterative training (Tables 5 and 7); and ComposeAE $+ \mathrm { G A }$ surpasses ComposeAE through gradient augmentation regularization for dataset augmentation and overfitting mitigation (Tables 7 and 9). These examples highlight the importance of further exploring advanced target matching strategies and implementing dataset augmentation techniques to enhance model generalization.

5.3.2 ZS-CIR. ZS-CIR models, including textual-inversion-based, pseudo-triplet-based, and training-free models, are evaluated on the FashionIQ, CIRR, and CIRCO datasets. We directly summarize their experimental results from the corresponding papers in Tables 10 and 11. It is worth noting that existing implementations of the ZS-CIR approach typically rely on the generalization capabilities of VLP-based encoders and are commonly tested with various backbone versions. Therefore, to enable a comprehensive comparison, we list all the performance results of methods utilizing different backbones in our tables. From these tables, we obtain the following observations. (1) Certain zero-shot methods yield comparable outcomes to some supervised methods. For example, on the FashionIQ-ori dataset, the top-performing ZS-CIR method, LinCIR $+ \mathrm { I P }$ -CIR (CLIP-G version), attains a score of 56.01 when averaged across the metrics. Significantly, it not only outperforms all of the traditional encoder-based supervised methods but also manages to achieve a performance comparable to nearly half the number of the VLP encoder-based supervised methods. This implies that even in the absence of manually annotated triplet data, one can still obtain satisfactory retrieval results by ingeniously devising the pre-training strategy and fully activating the potential of powerful VLP capabilities within the CIR context. (2) Typically, the same zero-shot methods, when equipped with a large backbone, consistently deliver better performance. For example, LinCIR, ISA, MagicLens, CompoDiff, LDRE, SEIZE, and WieMoCIR all confirm that their larger-scale variants perform more effectively. This further evidences that VLP encoders, which possess well-trained multimodal encoding and cross-modal retrieval capabilities, significantly influence the performance of ZS-CIR. (3) Generally, among the three types of zero-shot methods, the overall performance of pseudo-triplet-based and training-free methods outperforms that of textual-inversion-based methods. For example, on the FashionIQ-ori dataset, regarding the average recall for textualinversion-based methods, pseudo-triplet-based methods, and training-free methods, the number of methods showing a performance greater than $4 5 . 0 0 \%$ is 2, 9, and 6, respectively. On the CIRR dataset, based on $\mathrm { R @ 1 0 } > 7 5 \%$ , the corresponding numbers are 2, 6, and 6. And on the CIRCO dataset, based on $\mathrm { m A P } @ 1 0 > 3 0 \%$ , the numbers are 0, 3, and 5. This can be ascribed to the fact that the pseudo-triplet-based methods still construct triplet data that is most similar to the supervised training paradigm. Although this group of methods is somewhat resource-intensive during the pseudo-triplet construction stage, their overall performance is highly promising. As for training-free methods, they perfectly preserve the inherent cross-modal image retrieval capabilities of advanced VLP models without any fine-tuning that could cause performance degradation.

# 6 Discussion and Future Directions

Addressing CIR tasks requires joint reasoning over both visual and textual information, making it a particularly challenging endeavor. In this section, we first examine the inherent issues in supervised CIR and ZS-CIR, highlighting potential technological trends for each. We then explore related tasks in CIR.

# 6.1 On Supervised CIR

The future research directions for supervised CIR are presented below, prioritized according to their potential impact on advancing the field.

—LLM-Based Image-Text Fusion. While existing CIR methods have explored diverse imagetext fusion strategies, the adoption of LLMs/MLLMs for query encoding and fusion remains underexplored, with far-reaching implications for both research and practical applications. While early studies [83, 161, 242] demonstrate the potential of leveraging LLMs for query encoding and fusion, the fundamental tradeoff between enhancing visual-textual alignment and preserving generative reasoning capabilities poses a critical challenge [71]. This limitation is particularly consequential because reasoning remains essential for handling complex, realworld CIR queries, where users often combine multiple attributes or abstract concepts in their modifications. The degradation of LLMs' native reasoning abilities during fine-tuning could severely restrict their effectiveness in nuanced retrieval scenarios, ultimately limiting the practical utility of these advanced models. Moreover, since one of the most promising advantages of MLLMs is their inherent ability to handle multimodal tasks without taskspecific architectures, overcoming this limitation is not merely a technical refinement but a pivotal step that could determine whether LLM-based approaches can fulfill their potential as versatile, general-purpose retrieval systems capable of handling diverse and complex queries across domains. Developing advanced fine-tuning strategies that reconcile these competing objectives could unlock new capabilities in multimodal retrieval.

Benchmark Dataset Construction. Existing datasets impose significant constraints on the development of supervised CIR systems. (1) Narrow dataset scope. Existing datasets are predominantly limited to specific domains (e.g., fashion, birds). Models optimized for these narrow benchmarks often fail when faced with the diversity of real-world queries. (2) Annotationinduced false negatives. The standard annotation pipeline—first selects candidate image pairs and then describes their differences—inherently introduces false negatives by excluding many valid target images. Models trained on such data cannot achieve robust multimodal comprehension. While the open-domain dataset CIRR addresses false negatives, it introduces new limitations. (1) Previous studies [6, 146] have shown that modification texts frequently provide enough information on their own, enabling models to bypass visual reasoning entirely. This undermines the development of genuine compositional reasoning capabilities, threatening the fundamental premise of CIR. (2) The rigid one-to-one query-target pairings fail to capture real-world ambiguity where multiple images could satisfy a query. This limits the system's applicability in real-world scenarios that require returning diverse, plausible results. (3) The dataset's limited scale (36.5K triplets) prevents CIR models from benefiting from the performance improvements predicted by scaling laws. This puts CIR at risk of falling behind other multimodal tasks (e.g., image captioning, cross-modal retrieval) where billion-scale datasets have driven significant breakthroughs. Therefore, future datasets must: (i) embrace many-to-many mappings to reflect real-world retrieval ambiguity; (ii) decouple visual-textual dependencies in annotations to enforce true compositional learning; and (iii) include a large number of high-quality triplets. Future efforts should prioritize large-scale, open-domain datasets with diverse, compositionally challenging examples to advance robust and generalizable CIR systems.

Task Synergy. The CIR triplet (reference image, text, target image) shares fundamental similarities with text-based image editing [81, 219, 223] in both input structure and multimodal understanding requirements. Yet current approaches treat these tasks in isolation, neglecting their complementary strengths—a critical oversight that stifles progress in both fields. This intrinsic task duality enables powerful mutual reinforcement. First, retrieval-guided generation addresses a fundamental limitation of pure generative models: their tendency to hallucinate implausible details. Retrieved images serve as realistic anchors for generation [55], enabling retrieval-augmented synthesis that preserves distributional fidelity while avoiding common artifacts such as unrealistic attribute combinations. Second, generation-augmented retrieval tackles the data scarcity bottleneck in CIR. The generative process demands deep target understanding [130, 185], implicitly enhancing the retrieval's capacity to interpret nuanced query intent beyond literal descriptions. Meanwhile, synthesized images provide hard negatives to strengthen embedding discrimination. Most significantly, a unified approach would enable dynamic adaptation—retrieving existing matches when available while generating novel compositions for unmatched queries [136]. Thus, developing such a unified approach, which fully capitalizes on the synergistic potential of both paradigms, represents a pivotal research direction for advancing multimodal intelligence systems. Target Matching with Noisy Data. The compositional nature of CIR introduces significant challenges in dataset annotation, frequently resulting in noisy triplets across existing benchmarks. Noisy samples fundamentally compromise the learning process by reinforcing spurious correlations and diluting meaningful signals, leading to degraded retrieval performance in downstream applications. Current approaches to noise mitigation remain limited in their effectiveness, mainly relying on binary triplet classification with global thresholds. These methods exhibit two critical weaknesses. First, they propagate errors by misclassifying borderline cases—samples with partial validity get either fully included or discarded. Second, they fail to recognize that some "noisy" samples actually serve as beneficial hard negatives that could strengthen metric learning. Essentially, the fundamental challenge lies in distinguishing between harmful noise (which should be removed) and challenging but useful samples (which should be retained with appropriate weighting). Overall, this necessitates the development of advanced methods capable of precisely assessing sample utility and strategically incorporating it to enhance model robustness. — Target Matching with Biased Data. The limitation of target matching with biased data poses a significant challenge in the field of CIR, as it undermines models' ability to develop robust multimodal reasoning skills. When datasets contain overly specific annotations to avoid false negatives, models may exploit superficial text-image correlations rather than learning meaningful compositional relationships. This overfitting to spurious patterns not only limits generalization to real-world scenarios but also obscures the true goal of CIR: enabling nuanced understanding of both visual and textual components. Successfully mitigating this challenge would not only enhance CIR systems but also contribute to more reliable and generalizable multimodal learning frameworks. Addressing this issue through causal intervention methods could represent a critical advancement, as it would enable models to disentangle text bias from genuine reasoning. — Model Interpretability. As CIR models grow increasingly complex, their interpretability decreases, making it challenging to understand how decisions are made during the retrieval process. This lack of transparency poses significant challenges to improving models, as well as for building user trust, particularly in high-stakes applications such as healthcare or legal search. Future research directions could focus on designing models that integrate neural networks with interpretable components, such as attention mechanisms or rule-based systems, to provide clearer insights into model behavior. Furthermore, standardized evaluation metrics for interpretability and user studies assessing the effectiveness of explanations in real-world scenarios will be crucial for advancing this field. By prioritizing interpretability, the CIR community can foster more reliable and trustworthy retrieval systems.

# 6.2 On ZS-CIR

While significant progress has been achieved in the field of ZS-CIR, several challenges and open questions remain. Below we outline these key issues, organized by their anticipated impact on the field.

Pseudo Triplets Generation. Current methods for generating pseudo triplets rely on cascaded systems—captioning models compress rich visual data into limited textual descriptions, followed by LLMs producing modification text. This pipeline creates a fundamental bottleneck: critical visual details (e.g., textures, fine patterns) are lost. This information loss is further compounded when LLMs generate modifications, as they operate on already degraded input and tend to produce generic edits (e.g., "change the color to blue") rather than precise, context-aware adjustments (e.g., "make the floral embroidery denser but keep the silk texture"). The implications of this limitation are twofold. On one hand, the generated pseudo-triplets typically produce coarse-grained modifications, causing the trained model to struggle with real-world queries that demand fine-grained control. On the other hand, the uniformity of LLM-generated prompts introduces a synthetic bias: most modifications follow predictable syntactic patterns, whereas human queries exhibit diverse phrasing. Overcoming this limitation is pivotal for scaling CIR to real applications, where nuanced attribute control and diverse query phrasing are essential. Future work may resort to multimodal dense captioning that preserves fine-grained visual attributes, iterative refinement frameworks where LLMs interact with visual grounding models to progressively sharpen modifications based on feedback loops, and data augmentation to diversify prompt phrasing by simulating different user personas or linguistic styles. Future work must treat pseudo-triplet generation as a multimodal reasoning challenge, integrating dense captioning to preserve visual details, iterative refinement with visual grounding's feedback for information precision, and persona-driven augmentation to mimic human diversity. Without such advances, CIR systems will remain constrained by synthetic data artifacts.

MLLM-Based Training-Free Methods. Existing training-free methods predominantly utilize LLMs to summarize the caption of the reference image along with the modification text, thereby transforming the task into text-to-image retrieval. However, this introduces a semantic bottleneck—losing visual details during fusion—while inheriting captioning model biases. The emergence of MLLMs presents a paradigm-shifting opportunity to address these challenges. Unlike conventional LLM-based approaches, MLLMs can directly process and reason about visual content alongside textual modifications, preserving fine-grained visual details while interpreting compositional queries. This capability enables a more sophisticated understanding of multimodal queries. Future research directions could explore: (1) optimal prompting strategies to elicit MLLMs' visual reasoning capabilities for retrieval tasks; and (2) techniques to mitigate potential hallucination issues while maintaining the models' compositional understanding. The development of MLLM-based zero-shot CIR systems could ultimately bridge the gap between human-like visual understanding and scalable retrieval systems. This represents a potential breakthrough for real-world applications where training data is scarce but precise multimodal understanding is critical.

Advanced Textual Inversion. As aforementioned, although fine-grained textual inversion methods have demonstrated superior performance on CIR tasks by preserving local visual details during pseudo-token mappings, two fundamental challenges persist. First, there is a misalignment between training and inference objectives, where models are optimized for generic image-text alignment but evaluated on compositional retrieval tasks. Current methods treat CIR as a byproduct of general-purpose pre-training rather than a specialized task requiring thorough compositional reasoning. For instance, models may excel at matching "red shoes" but fail when queried with "shoes like these but redder," highlighting their inability to decompose and recompose attributes. Second, there is inevitable information loss in the current pseudo-token mappings. Current textual-inversion schemes tend to discard spatial relationships or subtle textures critical for complex queries (e.g., "a painting with brushstrokes like Van Gogh but in pastel colors"). Successfully addressing these two challenges could not only unlock more robust and generalizable CIR systems but also bridge the gap between generic vision-language pre-training and the specialized demands of CIR.

Few-Shot CIR. Current CIR approaches face a fundamental dilemma: supervised methods are constrained by dataset scarcity, while zero-shot methods completely ignore valuable labeled examples. In practice, annotating a small number of samples is often feasible. Recently, few-shot CIR [72, 197] emerges as a pragmatic middle ground, where strategically selected annotations can yield promising performance gains. The effectiveness of few-shot learning hinges on two critical factors: (1) ensuring diverse coverage of compositional operations (like color changes, texture modifications, and spatial adjustments); and (2) maximizing the instructional value of each annotated triplet—how well it teaches the model to handle various compositional queries. This insight suggests moving beyond random sampling toward active learning strategies that intelligently select informative examples. Important open questions remain, such as how to quantify the utility of potential samples or how selection strategies should adapt to different domains, like fashion and furniture design. Successfully addressing these challenges could enable highly efficient CIR systems that achieve complex compositional reasoning while requiring only minimal labeled examples—a crucial capability for practical deployment in data-scarce domains. — Efficacy-Efficiency Tradeoff. Current evaluation protocols prioritize retrieval efficacy while largely overlooking efficiency considerations. This narrow focus often comes at the expense of practical applicability. For instance, several approaches [71, 217, 218] employ multiple LLM reasoning passes over multimodal queries to improve retrieval accuracy, despite introducing prohibitive computational overhead. Achieving an optimal balance between effectiveness and efficiency is therefore critical, particularly for enabling real-world deployment. Two key research directions address this challenge: (1) developing knowledge distillation schemes to achieve competitive performance with lightweight architectures; and (2) incorporating accelerated retrieval techniques such as hash learning [184] and advanced index structure [199].

# 6.3 On Related Tasks of CIR

While current research has expanded CIR to various query formats, including attribute-based, sketch-based, remote sensing-based, dialog-based, and video-based queries, significant limitations persist. First, current approaches to CIR-related tasks remain underdeveloped, often failing to fully address the unique demands of specific sub-tasks like attribute-based, sketch-based, or video-based retrieval. Each of these tasks presents distinct challenges—from precise attribute disentanglement in fashion retrieval to temporal coherence in video retrieval—that require specialized solutions. This gap represents a significant research opportunity to develop more tailored approaches that better capture these domain-specific requirements. Second, beyond refining existing methods, the field stands to benefit greatly from exploring novel task formulations that push the boundaries of CIR. Potential directions include 3D-aware retrieval for product configuration or multi-agent collaborative retrieval systems. Such expansions could not only broaden CIR's practical applications but also deepen our theoretical understanding of cross-modal compositional reasoning. Most critically, the field urgently requires unified multimodal architectures with dynamic adaptation capabilities—systems that can process diverse input modalities (e.g., sketches, video clips, and textual attributes) through shared backbone networks while automatically specializing their attention mechanisms for task-specific requirements.

# 7 Conclusions

In this article, we provide a comprehensive review of state-of-the-art methods in CIR. Specifically, we begin by reviewing supervised CIR approaches, with a focus on the four main components of their general framework. We then explore the emerging field of zero-shot CIR, along with several related tasks in CIR. Following this, we review various CIR datasets, grouping results according to the datasets and providing corresponding analyses. Through these comprehensive endeavors, we have gained profound insights into the current challenges in CIR and have outlined some promising research directions for the future. This comprehensive survey is an invaluable and insightful resource for researchers and practitioners, significantly propelling the rapidly evolving field of CIR.
